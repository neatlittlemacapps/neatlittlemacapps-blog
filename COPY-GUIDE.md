# Frank De Graeve Copy Guide

## Core Voice Profile
Frank writes like he talks—conversational, opinionated, self-aware. He's not trying to impress; he's trying to engage. His tone is expert but never condescending, witty but not forced, Dutch-casual even when writing in English.

---

## DO's

**Opening Hooks (Conversation Starters)**
- Start with a genuine question or admission: "I generally only rant online 'bout once a year (I think)."
- Use relatable scenarios: "So I'm guessing you all have a smartphone?"
- Lead with the problem/tension, not the solution: "Why am I all up in arms here?"

**Sentence Structure**
- Mix short punchy sentences with longer explanations: "It's hard work. It requires re-energising continuously."
- Use fragments and casual conjunctions: "And yet these devices..." "So what?" "Well, some time ago..."
- Embrace rhetorical questions to move readers along
- Vary length—don't let paragraphs become predictable

**Addressing the Reader**
- Use "you" directly and often: "What about it? Well..." or "You know, the little speaker..."
- Parenthetical asides in conversational tone: "yes, from the movies" or "can't keep up with those numbers"
- Occasionally invite collaboration: "Let's roll back here and start at the beginning."

**Humor & Personality**
- Self-deprecating: "I suck at motion and visual design"
- Observation-based wit: "unicorn pony, you can do it all can't yah? With the rainbow and sprinkly things and such?!"
- Casual curse words when appropriate to the tone: "sneaky bastards"
- Knowing commentary on industry absurdities: "I only post here when I need something from you? Eh..."

**Technical Explanation**
- Simplify but don't oversimplify: Explain what USDZ means, but explain it quickly
- Use everyday analogies: "just like you have '.JPG' for images"
- Bridge jargon with accessibility: Define terms inline, then move forward
- Show you understand complexity without drowning readers in it

**Paragraph Length**
- Short paragraphs for emphasis or transitions
- Medium paragraphs for explanation (3-5 sentences)
- Mix in occasional longer paragraphs when building narrative momentum
- Use white space as a thinking tool

**Transitions & Flow**
- "So what?" "But wait..." "Here's the thing..." (conversational connectors)
- "Back to..." to refocus after a tangent
- Use section headers sparingly but meaningfully
- Create rhythm through varied pacing, not formula

**Punctuation & Style**
- Use ellipses (...) for trailing thoughts or pauses: "Fancy eh?"
- Parentheses for asides that feel natural, not academic
- Exclamation marks for genuine excitement, not forced energy
- Commas before conjunctions: His natural speech pattern
- Quotation marks for specific quotes, not scare quotes

**Call-to-Action (When Present)**
- Make it genuine and specific: "Let's discover the opportunities together"
- Avoid corporate jargon: "Let's do this" vs. "reach out to us"
- Position it as invitation, not demand
- Often tied to solving real problems he's identified

## DON'Ts

**HARD Avoids (AI Red Flags)**
- Em dashes (—) for dramatic pauses. Frank uses commas, ellipses, and parentheses
- "genuinely" as an intensifier
- "leverage" (use "use," "make," "create," "apply")
- "delve into" (use "dig into," "look at," "check out")
- "navigate" (use "deal with," "work through," "handle")
- "it's worth noting" (Frank just... notes it. Directly.)
- "I'm passionate about..." (Frank shows passion through action, not declaration)
- "in today's fast-paced world" (absolutely absent from his style)
- Repetitive parallel structure in lists (all starting the same way)
- "whether...or" constructions overused for artificial balance

**Tone Misfires**
- Overly formal: No "Furthermore" or "In conclusion"
- Aggressively casual where it doesn't fit: Frank matches energy to topic
- Forced inspirational language: "Your journey" or "Unleash your potential"
- Corporate speak: "synergize," "circle back," "take this offline"
- Talking AT readers instead of WITH them

**Structural Mistakes**
- Bullet points that are complete sentences (Frank uses them, but as fragments or conversational hits)
- Numbered lists for everything
- Excessive subheadings breaking up natural flow
- Paragraphs longer than 7-8 sentences without a break
- Opening with a generic statement (e.g., "In the modern digital landscape...")

**Voice Violations**
- "We believe..." as a corporate plural (Frank says "I" and owns it)
- Third-person references to himself (Never.)
- Hedging with "arguably" or "some might say"
- Apologizing for opinions: He states them with backing, not with caveats
- Humility-bragging disguised as self-awareness

**What's Absent = What to Avoid**
- Motivational quotes
- Case studies with "lessons learned" sections
- Repetitive value propositions
- Sentences starting with gerunds for style (ing-heavy openings)
- Clickbait energy despite engaging headlines
- Fake urgency or FOMO language

## Writing Checklist Before Publishing

- [ ] Can I read this aloud and it sounds like me talking to a friend?
- [ ] Did I start with a question or real tension, not a statement?
- [ ] Is there at least one moment where I'm genuine about what I don't know?
- [ ] Did I avoid all "leverage/delve/navigate/it's worth noting" patterns?
- [ ] No em dashes? Good.
- [ ] Is my humor tied to observation, not forced?
- [ ] Did I mix sentence lengths intentionally?
- [ ] Am I inviting the reader in, or lecturing them?
- [ ] Would I say this way out loud? If not, rewrite.

---

## The Essence

Frank's voice is **permission to be real**. He's smart but unpretentious, knowledgeable but curious, opinionated but open. He doesn't write like AI because he doesn't optimize for keywords or structure—he optimizes for genuine connection with people who get it. Write like you're explaining this to someone over coffee, with occasional tangled thoughts and a laugh when things get absurd.
